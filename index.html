Crisis De-escalation

Managing a High-Risk Elopement Attempt

I worked with a patient who was experiencing command hallucinations and actively attempting to leave the unit. Rather than blocking their path or immediately calling security, I maintained a safe distance and used reflective listening to acknowledge their distress. By validating their feelings with statements like "It sounds like you feel you need to leave to be safe right now," I was able to help them identify their underlying need for security. This approach successfully shifted the situation from an immediate elopement risk to a calmer discussion at the nurses' station, preventing the need for a Code Gray response while keeping the patient engaged with treatment.

Behavioral Intervention

Helping a Patient Re-engage with Group Therapy

I worked with a patient on the unit who had severe social anxiety and consistently avoided group sessions. Rather than insisting they attend immediately, I implemented a graduated exposure approach by breaking participation into smaller, achievable steps. We began with brief check-ins outside the group room, progressed to having them sit in the doorway to observe, and eventually worked up to participating for the first five minutes. Over three shifts, their attendance improved by 40%. This experience demonstrated how meeting patients where they are and building gradually can be more effective than direct confrontation.
